DPI reference — how cm maps to pixels

Because a screen counts everything in pixels, a centimetre figure has no fixed meaning until you decide on a DPI. The table below lists the values people rely on most.

About resizing images in cm

Use this page to bring an image to a centimetre size at 300 DPI. The logic behind it is straightforward: at 300 DPI a single centimetre is close to 118 px, so you feed those pixels into the resizer. It doesn't matter whether you're readying paperwork for an exam application, sizing a photo before printing, or matching an image to a precise measurement for some project — the maths is handled the moment you load it, right there in your browser.

There's nothing to download and nothing gets sent off anywhere; the picture never leaves the device you're on.

Handy 300 DPI conversions: 1 inch = 300 px · 1 cm ≈ 118 px · 1 mm ≈ 11.8 px.

Frequently asked questions

Does resizing an image in cm cost anything?
Not a rupee — the whole thing is free. You never register, nothing gets stamped with a watermark, and there's no ceiling on how many images you push through it.
Is my image kept private?
Fully. Every bit of the resizing is carried out on your own machine through HTML5 Canvas, which means the file is never uploaded to any server and stays on your device throughout.
Can I use it on a phone?
Absolutely. Pull up YESPYQ in Chrome or Safari on any iPhone or Android handset, load your picture and save the output — there's no app to download.
Which image formats can I upload?
You can bring in JPG, JPEG, PNG, WEBP and HEIC/HEIF files wherever the browser can read them. What comes back is always a JPEG, so it slots straight into Indian government exam and ID portals.
Is any software installation required?
Nothing at all. The whole tool lives inside your browser — Chrome, Firefox, Edge or Safari — and behaves the same on desktop and mobile.
Which DPI is right for printing?
When you're printing the usual things — ID cards, exam photos, documents — lean on 300 DPI as the benchmark: 1 inch = 300 px, 1 cm ≈ 118 px, 1 mm ≈ 11.8 px. Since the resizer deals purely in pixels, plug in the pixel figures that line up.
Why does it save as JPEG rather than PNG?
JPEG is what nearly every Indian government exam and ID portal asks for. On top of that it packs down to a fraction of a PNG's size while holding photo quality, which makes it a natural fit for form uploads.
