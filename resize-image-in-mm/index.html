DPI reference — how mm maps to pixels

Files are measured in pixels, which means a figure in millimetres is only nailed down after you set a DPI. The values people reach for most are shown here.

About resizing images in mm

Feed it a measurement in millimetres and it handles the resize at 300 DPI. Keep one figure in mind — 10 mm comes to about 118 px at 300 DPI — then type the matching pixels in. It slots in wherever you need it: readying paperwork for an exam form, prepping a photo for print, or matching a picture to one precise size on a project, all finished in your browser in a heartbeat.

Frequently asked questions

Does resizing an image in mm cost anything?
Not one paisa — you never register, no watermark is added, and there is no ceiling on how many images you can run.
Is my image kept private?
Wholly. The resize is carried out by HTML5 Canvas inside your browser, so the image is never handed to a server and never leaves the device you are on.
Can I use it on a phone?
Yes, easily. Open YESPYQ in Chrome or Safari on any iPhone or Android, load your image, and download the result — there is no app to fetch.
Which image formats can I upload?
Bring JPG, JPEG, PNG, WEBP or HEIC/HEIF (so long as your browser handles it). The output is always JPEG, which sits best with Indian government exam and ID portals.
Is any software installation required?
None at all. It runs completely within the browser you already use — Chrome, Firefox, Edge or Safari — on desktop or mobile.
Which DPI is right for printing?
Go with 300 DPI for the usual prints — ID cards, exam photos, documents: 1 inch = 300 px, 1 cm ≈ 118 px, 1 mm ≈ 11.8 px. Since the tool measures in exact pixels, feed in the pixel numbers these give.
Why does it save as JPEG rather than PNG?
Almost every Indian government exam and ID portal asks for JPEG, and it keeps files far lighter than PNG without dropping photo quality — just right for online form uploads.
