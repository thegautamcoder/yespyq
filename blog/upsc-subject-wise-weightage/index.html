UPSC Prelims Subject-Wise Weightage & Trend Analysis

By YESPYQ · Updated June 2026 · 9 min read

UPSC Prelims GS Paper-I has 100 questions. Where those questions come from is remarkably consistent year to year — which means you can plan your preparation around the data instead of fear. This article breaks down the approximate subject-wise weightage and what the trend means for how you study.

Approximate weightage in GS Paper-I

Exact counts vary, but over the last decade the distribution has settled into a recognisable band. Treat these as planning ranges, not guarantees:

SubjectTypical questionsTrend
Polity & Governance15–20Stable, conceptual
Environment & Ecology12–18Rising
Economy13–18Stable, application-heavy
History & Art/Culture12–17Culture rising
Geography10–15Stable, map-based
Science & Technology8–12Current-affairs linked
Current AffairsCuts across allStatic-linked

What the trend tells you

Environment is now a top-tier subject

A decade ago environment was an afterthought. Today it rivals Polity in volume, and it is high-yield because the questions are often factual and learnable. If you are short on time, environment offers some of the best return per hour.

Polity is the most reliable scorer

Polity questions are conceptual and repeat in spirit year after year, which is why we cover the most repeated Polity themes separately. A strong grip here is the closest thing to guaranteed marks.

Economy and Science lean on current affairs

You cannot prepare these purely from static books. They reward linking the news to fundamentals — see our current affairs approach.

Weightage tells you where the marks are. PYQ analysis tells you how those marks are asked. You need both.

How to convert weightage into a study plan

  1. Front-load the heavyweights. Polity, Environment, Economy and History together make up the bulk of the paper. Secure these before chasing niche areas.
  2. Match effort to volatility. Stable subjects (Polity, Geography) reward deep one-time mastery; volatile ones (Science, Current Affairs) reward continuous light touch.
  3. Validate with PYQs. After studying a subject, solve its PYQ set to confirm you are at the right depth — not too shallow, not over-prepared.

The mistake of chasing balance

Many aspirants try to be equally strong everywhere. The data says otherwise: a candidate who is excellent in the top four subjects and merely competent elsewhere will usually beat someone who is uniformly average. Specialise where the volume is, then broaden.

Frequently asked questions

Which subject has the most questions in UPSC Prelims?

Polity, Environment and Economy typically lead, each contributing roughly 13–20 questions in a given year. Together with History they form the high-weight core of GS Paper-I.

Is the weightage the same every year?

No — it fluctuates within a band. That is exactly why you prepare ranges rather than fixed counts, and why solving multiple years of PYQs is essential.
