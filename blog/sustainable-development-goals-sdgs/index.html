Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

By YESPYQ · Updated July 2026 · 5 min read
Quick answer

The SDGs are 17 global goals with 169 targets, adopted by UN members in 2015 under the 2030 Agenda. They succeeded the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) and, unlike them, apply to all countries and integrate the economic, social and environmental dimensions of development. India tracks progress through NITI Aayog's SDG India Index.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are a set of 17 interconnected global goals adopted by all United Nations member states in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. Described as a 'blueprint to achieve a better and more sustainable future for all', they address the world's most pressing challenges — from poverty and hunger to climate change, inequality and peace — and set a shared target date of 2030.

From MDGs to SDGs

The SDGs succeeded the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), which ran from 2000 to 2015 and focused mainly on developing countries. The SDGs are broader and more ambitious: they were negotiated through an inclusive global process, apply universally to all nations (rich and poor), and cover a much wider range of issues. This shift reflected the understanding that development challenges are interconnected and require action everywhere, not just in the developing world.

The 17 goals

The 17 goals cover: no poverty; zero hunger; good health and well-being; quality education; gender equality; clean water and sanitation; affordable and clean energy; decent work and economic growth; industry, innovation and infrastructure; reduced inequalities; sustainable cities and communities; responsible consumption and production; climate action; life below water; life on land; peace, justice and strong institutions; and partnerships for the goals. Each goal has specific targets — 169 in all — with indicators to measure progress.

The three dimensions and '5 Ps'

The SDGs integrate the three dimensions of sustainable development — economic, social and environmental — recognising that they are inseparable. They are often summarised around the '5 Ps': People (ending poverty and hunger), Planet (protecting the environment), Prosperity (ensuring prosperous, fulfilling lives), Peace (fostering peaceful, just societies) and Partnership (mobilising global cooperation). The guiding pledge of the 2030 Agenda is to 'leave no one behind'.

India's role and initiatives

India played an active role in shaping the 2030 Agenda, and many of its concerns are reflected in the goals. NITI Aayog is the nodal body coordinating the SDGs in India and publishes the SDG India Index, which ranks states and Union Territories on their progress, fostering competitive federalism. Numerous national schemes — on poverty, health, sanitation (Swachh Bharat), energy, education and financial inclusion — are aligned with specific SDGs, making India's development programmes central to global goal achievement given its large population.

Progress and challenges

Progress on the SDGs has been uneven and was set back by shocks like the COVID-19 pandemic, conflicts and economic stress, leaving the world off-track on many goals as the 2030 deadline approaches. Key challenges include mobilising adequate finance, especially for developing countries, bridging data gaps to measure progress, and managing trade-offs (for instance between rapid growth and environmental protection). Accelerating action in the remaining years is a major focus of global summits.

Why the SDGs matter

The SDGs provide a common language and framework for governments, businesses and civil society to align their efforts towards sustainability. They are non-binding, relying on national ownership and voluntary reviews rather than enforcement, which is both a strength (flexibility) and a weakness (no penalties for inaction). For a country like India, balancing the aspiration to grow with the need to develop sustainably and inclusively lies at the heart of the SDG agenda.

Why it matters for UPSC

The number of SDGs (17) and targets (169), the 2030 Agenda, the transition from the MDGs, the '5 Ps' and the NITI Aayog SDG India Index are commonly tested in Prelims, and the SDGs feature widely in Mains answers on development, environment and governance.

Key takeaways

  • The SDGs are 17 goals with 169 targets, adopted in 2015 under the 2030 Agenda.
  • They succeeded the Millennium Development Goals (2000-2015) and apply to all countries.
  • They integrate the economic, social and environmental dimensions of development.
  • They are often summarised by the '5 Ps': People, Planet, Prosperity, Peace, Partnership.
  • The guiding principle is to 'leave no one behind'.
  • NITI Aayog coordinates the SDGs in India and publishes the SDG India Index.

Frequently asked questions

How many Sustainable Development Goals are there?

There are 17 SDGs with 169 targets, adopted in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.

Who tracks SDG progress across Indian states?

NITI Aayog, through the SDG India Index, which ranks states and Union Territories on their performance.

What is the difference between the MDGs and the SDGs?

The MDGs (2000-2015) focused mainly on developing countries and had 8 goals; the SDGs (2015-2030) are universal, apply to all countries, and have 17 goals covering a wider range of issues.

What are the '5 Ps' of the SDGs?

People, Planet, Prosperity, Peace and Partnership — a summary of the core themes of the 2030 Agenda.

By when are the SDGs to be achieved?

By 2030, the target year of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.

Who adopted the Sustainable Development Goals?

All 193 United Nations member states adopted them at the UN Sustainable Development Summit in September 2015.
