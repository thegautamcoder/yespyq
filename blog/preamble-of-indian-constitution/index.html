Preamble of the Indian Constitution

By YESPYQ · Updated July 2026 · 5 min read
Quick answer

The words 'Socialist', 'Secular' and 'Integrity' were added by the 42nd Amendment (1976). In Kesavananda Bharati (1973), the Supreme Court held it is part of the Constitution and can be amended, subject to the basic structure.

The Preamble is the introductory statement of the Constitution that sets out its guiding ideals, the source of its authority and its objectives. Based on the Objectives Resolution moved by Jawaharlal Nehru and adopted by the Constituent Assembly, it is often described as the 'identity card' or 'soul' of the Constitution, and serves as a key to interpreting its provisions when their language is ambiguous.

Key words and their meaning

The Preamble declares India a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic and resolves to secure to all citizens Justice (social, economic, political), Liberty (of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship), Equality (of status and opportunity), and to promote Fraternity assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the nation. The words 'Socialist', 'Secular' and 'Integrity' were added later by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976.

Source of authority

The opening phrase 'We, the People of India' establishes that the ultimate source of the Constitution's authority is the people of India themselves, embodying the principle of popular sovereignty. The closing words — 'do hereby adopt, enact and give to ourselves this Constitution' — reinforce that the Constitution is self-given, not granted by any external power.

Is the Preamble part of the Constitution?

This question has evolved through case law. In the Berubari Union case (1960), the Supreme Court held the Preamble was not part of the Constitution. This was overruled in Kesavananda Bharati (1973), where the Court held that the Preamble is a part of the Constitution, can be used to interpret it, and can be amended under Article 368 — but without destroying its basic structure. The Preamble has been amended only once, by the 42nd Amendment.

Significance

The Preamble is non-justiciable — it is not directly enforceable in courts and grants no powers by itself — but it reflects the philosophy and fundamental values of the Constitution. Courts frequently rely on it to resolve ambiguities, and it anchors doctrines like secularism and democracy that are treated as part of the basic structure.

Meaning of the key terms

Each keyword carries deep meaning. 'Sovereign' means India is internally and externally free, subject to no external authority. 'Socialist' — added in 1976 — refers to democratic socialism and a mixed economy where public and private sectors coexist, aiming to reduce inequality, rather than communist state ownership. 'Secular' means the State has no official religion and treats all faiths equally, a principle the Supreme Court affirmed as part of the basic structure in the S.R. Bommai case (1994). 'Democratic' covers not just political democracy (universal adult franchise, elected government) but also social and economic democracy. 'Republic' means the head of state — the President — is elected, not hereditary.

The objectives — Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity

The Preamble's ideals were drawn from the objectives of the French Revolution and the ideals of the Indian freedom struggle. Justice is envisaged in three forms — social, economic and political — inspired by the Russian Revolution. Liberty guarantees freedom of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship. Equality assures equal status and opportunity to all citizens. Fraternity promotes a sense of brotherhood, assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the nation. Together, these four ideals are meant to be understood not in isolation but as a 'trinity' that sustains a just and inclusive society.

Why it matters for UPSC

The Preamble's keywords and their order, the 42nd Amendment additions, the shift from Berubari to Kesavananda on whether it is part of the Constitution, and its amendability are among the most frequently examined Polity facts in Prelims.

Key takeaways

  • The Preamble is based on the Objectives Resolution moved by Nehru.
  • It declares India a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic.
  • 'Socialist', 'Secular' and 'Integrity' were added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976.
  • 'We, the People of India' embodies popular sovereignty.
  • Kesavananda Bharati (1973) held it is part of the Constitution and amendable, subject to the basic structure; it has been amended only once.

Frequently asked questions

Which words were added to the Preamble by the 42nd Amendment?

'Socialist', 'Secular' and 'Integrity' were added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976.

Can the Preamble be amended?

Yes. The Supreme Court held in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) that the Preamble is part of the Constitution and can be amended under Article 368, subject to the basic structure. It has been amended once, in 1976.

On which resolution is the Preamble based?

The Preamble is based on the Objectives Resolution, drafted and moved by Jawaharlal Nehru and adopted by the Constituent Assembly.

Is the Preamble justiciable?

No. The Preamble is non-justiciable and grants no powers by itself, but it is used by courts to interpret the Constitution.

What does 'Secular' in the Preamble mean?

That India has no official state religion and treats all religions equally — a principle affirmed as part of the basic structure in the S.R. Bommai case (1994).

Why is India called a 'Republic' in the Preamble?

Because the head of state, the President, is elected and holds office for a fixed term, rather than being a hereditary monarch.
