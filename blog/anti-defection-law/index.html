Anti-Defection Law

The Anti-Defection Law was introduced by the 52nd Constitutional Amendment, 1985, adding the Tenth Schedule to curb political defections that destabilised governments.

Grounds for disqualification

A member can be disqualified if they voluntarily give up party membership or vote against the party whip. The law applies to members of Parliament and state legislatures.

The Speaker's role

The presiding officer (Speaker or Chairman) decides on disqualification. Courts can review this decision, and the Supreme Court has stressed that decisions should be made within a reasonable time.

Exceptions

The original provision allowed mergers if two-thirds of a legislative party agreed. The earlier 'split' exception was removed by the 91st Amendment, 2003.

Why it matters for UPSC

The Tenth Schedule — its amendment number, grounds for disqualification and the Speaker's deciding role — is regularly tested, especially in current-affairs-linked questions.

Frequently asked questions

Which amendment introduced the Anti-Defection Law?

The 52nd Constitutional Amendment, 1985, which added the Tenth Schedule.

Who decides disqualification under the Anti-Defection Law?

The presiding officer of the House (the Speaker or Chairman), subject to judicial review.
