Election Commission of India (ECI)

By YESPYQ · Updated July 2026 · 6 min read
Quick answer

The Election Commission of India (ECI) is a constitutional body under Article 324 that superintends, directs and controls elections to Parliament, state legislatures and the offices of President and Vice-President. It is a three-member body (Chief Election Commissioner plus two Election Commissioners) and enforces the Model Code of Conduct.

The Election Commission of India (ECI) is an autonomous, permanent constitutional authority responsible for administering elections in India. Established on 25 January 1950 (now marked as National Voters' Day), it is the guardian of free and fair elections — the bedrock of India's democracy. It oversees the world's largest democratic exercise, managing an electorate of over 96 crore voters across national and state elections.

Constitutional basis and powers

The ECI is established under Article 324 of the Constitution, which vests in it the superintendence, direction and control of the entire electoral process. It conducts elections to the Lok Sabha, the Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies and Councils, and to the offices of the President and Vice-President of India. Its powers are drawn from the Constitution as well as laws like the Representation of the People Acts of 1950 and 1951.

Composition

Originally a single-member body, the ECI has been a multi-member body since 1993. It now consists of the Chief Election Commissioner (CEC) and two Election Commissioners, all appointed by the President. They enjoy equal powers, and decisions are taken by majority. All hold office for a term of six years or until the age of 65, whichever is earlier.

Independence and removal

To insulate it from executive pressure, the Constitution protects the CEC's tenure: the CEC can be removed only through the same rigorous process as a Supreme Court judge (by Parliament on grounds of proven misbehaviour or incapacity). The two Election Commissioners can be removed only on the recommendation of the CEC. Their service conditions cannot be varied to their disadvantage after appointment — safeguards designed to guarantee the Commission's independence.

Key functions

The ECI prepares and periodically revises electoral rolls, notifies the schedule of elections and conducts the entire poll, recognises and registers political parties, allots election symbols and resolves disputes over them, and grants recognition as national or state parties. It also advises on the disqualification of legislators and sets limits on election expenditure to keep the process fair.

The Model Code of Conduct

One of the ECI's most powerful instruments is the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) — a set of norms for the conduct of political parties and candidates once elections are announced. Though not a statutory law, it is enforced strictly to ensure a level playing field: it restricts governments from announcing new schemes or transfers, and bars the use of official machinery or communal appeals for votes. The MCC comes into force from the date of the poll announcement until results are declared.

Challenges and reforms

The ECI has driven major reforms — Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs), Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT), photo electoral rolls, NOTA (None of the Above), and voter-awareness (SVEEP) programmes. Yet it faces ongoing debates: over the transparency of the appointment process (a 2023 Supreme Court judgment and a new law reshaped the selection panel), the role of money and 'freebies' in elections, electoral bonds and political funding, and the credibility of EVMs — making electoral reform a live and important theme.

Why it matters for UPSC

Article 324, the ECI's composition (a three-member body since 1993), its constitutional protections, and its enforcement of the Model Code of Conduct are recurring Prelims and Mains themes, especially around election cycles and appointment-related news.

Key takeaways

  • The ECI is a constitutional body under Article 324, established in 1950.
  • It is a three-member body — the CEC and two Election Commissioners — appointed by the President.
  • The CEC can be removed only like a Supreme Court judge, protecting independence.
  • It conducts national and state elections and President/VP polls, but NOT local body elections.
  • The Model Code of Conduct, though not a law, is strictly enforced from the poll announcement.
  • Reforms include EVMs, VVPAT and NOTA; appointment transparency and political funding are live debates.

Frequently asked questions

Under which article is the Election Commission established?

Article 324 of the Constitution of India.

Does the ECI conduct local body elections?

No. Local body (panchayat and municipal) elections are conducted by the State Election Commissions, not the ECI.

How many members does the Election Commission have?

It is a three-member body — the Chief Election Commissioner and two Election Commissioners — a structure in place since 1993.

What is the Model Code of Conduct?

A set of norms for political parties and candidates, enforced by the ECI from the announcement of elections until results, to ensure a free and fair, level playing field.

How is the Chief Election Commissioner removed?

The CEC can be removed only through the same process as a Supreme Court judge — by Parliament on grounds of proven misbehaviour or incapacity.

Which laws govern the conduct of elections in India?

Chiefly the Representation of the People Act, 1950 (electoral rolls and seat allocation) and the Representation of the People Act, 1951 (conduct of elections, qualifications, disqualifications and offences), alongside the constitutional powers under Article 324.

When was National Voters' Day started and why?

National Voters' Day is observed on 25 January every year, marking the ECI's foundation day in 1950, to encourage citizens — especially new young voters — to enrol and participate in elections.
