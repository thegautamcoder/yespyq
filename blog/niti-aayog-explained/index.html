NITI Aayog

By YESPYQ · Updated July 2026 · 6 min read
Quick answer

NITI Aayog, created by an executive resolution in 2015, replaced the Planning Commission. It is neither a constitutional nor a statutory body and does not allocate funds. Chaired by the Prime Minister, it acts as the government's premier policy think tank and champions cooperative and competitive federalism.

The National Institution for Transforming India (NITI Aayog) was set up on 1 January 2015 to replace the Planning Commission, which had existed since 1950. The move marked a shift away from top-down, five-year central planning towards a collaborative, advisory 'think tank' model that gives states a bigger voice in shaping national development — reflecting the principle that India's growth must be driven from the states upward.

Nature and structure

NITI Aayog is neither a constitutional nor a statutory body — it was created by a Union Cabinet executive resolution, not by an Act of Parliament or the Constitution. The Prime Minister is its chairperson. Its structure includes a Vice-Chairperson (of Cabinet rank), a Governing Council comprising all Chief Ministers and Lieutenant Governors of UTs, regional councils, full-time and part-time members, ex-officio members, special invitees and a Chief Executive Officer (CEO) appointed by the PM.

From allocation to advice

The most important change from the Planning Commission is that NITI Aayog does not allocate funds to states or ministries — that role now rests largely with the Finance Ministry and the Finance Commission. Instead, NITI Aayog acts as the government's premier policy think tank, providing strategic and technical advice, designing long-term visions and strategies, and fostering a 'Team India' spirit of shared national development.

Cooperative and competitive federalism

NITI Aayog promotes cooperative federalism by involving states as equal partners in policy design through the Governing Council and consultations, rather than dictating plans from Delhi. It also drives competitive federalism by publishing indices — on health, education, water management, innovation, export preparedness and the SDGs — that rank states and UTs, encouraging them to compete and learn from one another.

Key initiatives

Beyond advice, NITI Aayog runs flagship efforts such as the Aspirational Districts Programme (which targets the most under-developed districts on health, education and infrastructure), the SDG India Index tracking progress on the Sustainable Development Goals, and various sectoral strategy documents and reforms. It has no power to impose these on states, relying instead on persuasion and data.

Why the Planning Commission was replaced

The Planning Commission, set up in 1950, framed India's Five-Year Plans and allocated central funds to states, operating in an era of a centrally-planned, mixed economy. Over the decades it drew criticism for a 'one-size-fits-all' approach, for treating states as passive recipients rather than partners, and for wielding enormous discretionary power over fund transfers. As India shifted to a market-led, liberalised economy after 1991 and states demanded greater say, the top-down planning model looked increasingly outdated. NITI Aayog was created to reflect this new reality — a facilitator and knowledge hub rather than a controller of purse strings.

The seven pillars and hubs

NITI Aayog articulates its approach through pillars of effective governance — including being pro-people, pro-activity, participative, empowering, inclusive of all groups, and ensuring equality and transparency. Internally it is organised around two hubs: the 'Team India Hub', which acts as the interface between states and the Centre, and the 'Knowledge and Innovation Hub', which builds NITI Aayog's role as a repository of research and best practices. This structure reflects its dual mandate of coordinating with states while generating cutting-edge policy ideas.

Major reports and platforms

NITI Aayog has produced influential documents and platforms — a Three-Year Action Agenda, a Seven-Year Strategy and a Fifteen-Year Vision document replacing the earlier Five-Year Plans, the National Multidimensional Poverty Index, the health, school-education, water and export-preparedness indices, and Atal Innovation Mission to promote a culture of innovation and entrepreneurship. It also serves as the nodal body for initiatives such as digital transformation, electric mobility and, at times, the monitoring of specific national programmes.

Criticisms and debates

While praised for promoting federalism and data-driven policy, NITI Aayog has also faced criticism. Some argue that by losing the power to allocate funds, it has become a body with influence but little authority, dependent entirely on the government's willingness to accept its advice. Others note that its indices, though useful for competition, can oversimplify complex state realities, and that a genuine think tank should be more independent of the executive. These debates make NITI Aayog a rich subject for Mains questions on institutions and governance reform.

Composition in detail

NITI Aayog's full composition includes the Prime Minister as chairperson; a Vice-Chairperson appointed by the PM and enjoying Cabinet rank; a Governing Council comprising the Chief Ministers of all states and Lieutenant Governors/Administrators of Union Territories; Regional Councils formed to address issues affecting specific regions, chaired by the PM or a nominee; full-time members; part-time members drawn from universities and research bodies; ex-officio members (up to four Union ministers nominated by the PM); special invitees who are experts and specialists; and a Chief Executive Officer of the rank of Secretary, appointed by the Prime Minister for a fixed tenure. This wide composition is designed to bring together political leadership, domain expertise and administrative capacity.

Cooperative federalism in action

NITI Aayog operationalises cooperative federalism in concrete ways. It involves states in formulating national strategies, hosts the Governing Council where Chief Ministers debate shared priorities, and has taken up subjects like agricultural marketing reform, health and water management in consultation with states. During national crises and reform pushes, it has acted as a coordinating platform between the Centre and states. At the same time, its competitive-federalism indices publicly rank states, which has nudged laggards to improve on measurable outcomes. Critics counter that real fiscal power still lies with the Union Finance Ministry, so cooperative federalism through NITI Aayog remains more consultative than binding — a tension frequently explored in answer-writing on Centre-state relations.

Why it matters for UPSC

NITI Aayog vs Planning Commission is a classic comparison — its 2015 creation, non-constitutional/non-statutory nature, the fact that it does not allocate funds, its structure (PM as chair, Governing Council of CMs) and its cooperative/competitive federalism role are commonly asked in Prelims and provide strong Mains material on governance and Centre-state relations. Its indices, the Aspirational Districts Programme and the shift from Five-Year Plans to Vision documents are also useful, ready-made examples that strengthen answers on institutions, federalism and outcome-based governance.

Key takeaways

  • NITI Aayog replaced the Planning Commission on 1 January 2015.
  • It is an executive body — neither constitutional nor statutory.
  • It does NOT allocate funds; it is purely advisory (a think tank).
  • The PM chairs it; the Governing Council includes all CMs and UT Lt. Governors.
  • It promotes cooperative federalism (states as partners) and competitive federalism (ranking indices).
  • Flagship work includes the Aspirational Districts Programme and the SDG India Index.

Frequently asked questions

Is NITI Aayog a constitutional body?

No. NITI Aayog is neither constitutional nor statutory; it was created by a Union Cabinet executive resolution on 1 January 2015.

Does NITI Aayog allocate funds to states?

No. Unlike the Planning Commission, NITI Aayog is an advisory think tank and does not allocate funds; fund allocation is handled by the Finance Ministry and Finance Commission.

Who is the chairperson of NITI Aayog?

The Prime Minister of India is the ex-officio chairperson of NITI Aayog.

What is the difference between NITI Aayog and the Planning Commission?

The Planning Commission was a top-down body that framed five-year plans and allocated funds; NITI Aayog is a bottom-up advisory think tank that does not allocate funds and emphasises cooperative and competitive federalism.
