National Education Policy 2020 (NEP 2020)

By YESPYQ · Updated July 2026 · 7 min read
Quick answer

NEP 2020 is India's new education policy (replacing the 1986 policy). It introduces a 5+3+3+4 school structure, mother-tongue instruction till at least Grade 5, a multidisciplinary higher-education model, and targets a 50% Gross Enrolment Ratio in higher education by 2035.

The National Education Policy 2020 (NEP) is India's first comprehensive education policy in over three decades, replacing the National Policy on Education of 1986. Approved by the Union Cabinet in July 2020 and drafted by a committee headed by Dr. K. Kasturirangan, it lays out a roadmap to transform schooling and higher education by 2040. Its guiding themes are flexibility, foundational learning, the use of Indian languages, and a shift from rote learning towards conceptual understanding, critical thinking and skills.

The 5+3+3+4 school structure

NEP replaces the old 10+2 structure with a 5+3+3+4 design — the Foundational stage (ages 3–8), Preparatory (8–11), Middle (11–14) and Secondary (14–18) stages. Crucially, it brings early childhood care and education (ages 3–6) into the formal school system for the first time, recognising that over 85% of a child's brain development occurs before age six.

Language and foundational literacy

The policy stresses the mother tongue, local or regional language as the medium of instruction at least until Grade 5 (preferably till Grade 8), while retaining flexibility. It sets universal Foundational Literacy and Numeracy (FLN) as an urgent national mission, operationalised through the NIPUN Bharat programme, aiming for every child to attain basic reading and arithmetic by Grade 3.

A flexible, multidisciplinary curriculum

NEP reduces curriculum content to core essentials, removes rigid separations between arts, science, commerce and vocational streams, and introduces vocational exposure with internships from Grade 6. Board exams are to be made 'low-stakes', and a new National Curriculum Framework and 360-degree holistic progress card replace one-time high-pressure assessment.

Higher education reforms

For higher education, NEP proposes a broad-based multidisciplinary approach, a single overarching regulator — the Higher Education Commission of India (HECI) — replacing bodies like the UGC and AICTE, multiple entry and exit points backed by an Academic Bank of Credits, and a four-year undergraduate degree with research. It targets raising the Gross Enrolment Ratio in higher education from around 27% to 50% by 2035.

Targets and implementation

The policy aims to raise public spending on education to 6% of GDP, set up institutions like the National Research Foundation to fund research, and internationalise education by allowing top foreign universities to operate in India. As education is on the Concurrent List, implementation depends on cooperation between the Centre and the states.

The four-year degree and Academic Bank of Credits

In higher education, NEP introduces a flexible undergraduate structure with multiple entry and exit points: a certificate after one year, a diploma after two, a bachelor's degree after three, and a four-year degree 'with research' for those who continue. Credits earned are digitally stored in an Academic Bank of Credits (ABC), so a student can pause and resume studies or transfer between institutions without losing progress. The long-term vision is to move towards large multidisciplinary universities and autonomous colleges, gradually phasing out the standalone 'affiliation' system and giving institutions graded autonomy.

Teachers, assessment and PARAKH

NEP places teachers at the centre of reform. It proposes a four-year integrated Bachelor of Education (B.Ed.) as the minimum qualification for school teachers by 2030, merit-based recruitment, and National Professional Standards for Teachers. On assessment, the policy shifts from high-stakes rote testing towards competency-based, formative evaluation that tests understanding and application. A new national assessment centre, PARAKH, is tasked with setting norms and standards, and school students are to take census-based assessments at Grades 3, 5 and 8 to track learning outcomes.

Technology, inclusion and Indian languages

A National Educational Technology Forum (NETF) is envisaged to guide the use of technology, alongside digital platforms like DIKSHA for e-content. The policy gives special attention to Socio-Economically Disadvantaged Groups (SEDGs), proposing a Gender-Inclusion Fund and Special Education Zones in disadvantaged regions to widen access. It strongly promotes Indian languages, the study of classical languages and literature, the standardisation of Indian Sign Language, and the setting up of institutions to strengthen Sanskrit and other languages — reflecting its emphasis on rootedness in Indian culture alongside global competence.

Challenges and criticisms

Despite wide praise, NEP faces real hurdles. The 6% of GDP spending goal has been an aspiration since the 1968 policy and remains unmet, raising doubts about funding. Critics worry that a single regulator like HECI could centralise control, that the three-language formula could reignite language politics in some states, and that ambitious targets such as universal foundational literacy by 2025 or a 50% GER by 2035 require enormous infrastructure and teacher capacity. Because education is a Concurrent subject, uneven adoption across states could widen rather than narrow disparities — making implementation, not vision, the real test of NEP 2020.

Why it matters for UPSC

NEP 2020 is heavily tested in both Prelims (factual features like the 5+3+3+4 structure, the GER target, the 6% of GDP goal, HECI) and Mains (education reform, cooperative federalism, the medium-of-instruction debate). It also recurs in current affairs whenever implementation steps — new curriculum frameworks, NIPUN Bharat targets or foreign-university rules — are announced.

Key takeaways

  • NEP 2020 replaces the 1986 policy and was drafted by the Kasturirangan committee.
  • The 5+3+3+4 structure formally brings ages 3–6 (early childhood) into schooling.
  • Mother-tongue instruction is recommended till at least Grade 5; FLN is delivered via NIPUN Bharat.
  • Higher education moves to a multidisciplinary model with HECI, an Academic Bank of Credits and a 50% GER target by 2035.
  • The policy aims for public education spending of 6% of GDP.

Frequently asked questions

What is the new school structure under NEP 2020?

NEP 2020 replaces 10+2 with a 5+3+3+4 structure covering ages 3 to 18 — Foundational (3–8), Preparatory (8–11), Middle (11–14) and Secondary (14–18) — bringing early childhood care and education into the formal system.

What is the higher-education GER target of NEP 2020?

NEP 2020 aims to raise the Gross Enrolment Ratio in higher education to 50% by 2035.

Which body will replace the UGC and AICTE under NEP 2020?

The policy proposes a single regulator, the Higher Education Commission of India (HECI), with separate verticals for regulation, accreditation, funding and academic standards.

How much of GDP does NEP 2020 want spent on education?

NEP 2020 recommends raising public investment in education to 6% of GDP.
